Management of type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

DEFINITION

Disorder of metabolism mainly carbohydrate characterized by non-fasting blood glucose greater than 200 mg/dL.

CRITERIA FOR DIAGNOSIS OF DIABETES

Fasting plasma glucose level >126 mg/dL( fasting: no caloric intake for at least 8 hours)*

OR

2-hour oral glucose tolerance test reading >200 mg/dL( described by WHO. a glucose load containing  equivalent of 75 g  glucose dissolved in water or 1.75 g/kg of body weight if weight is <18 kg) *

OR

HbA1c>6.5%(Test is performed in a laboratory using a method that is NGSP certified and standardized to DCCT)*

Classification and Diagnosis of Diabetes: Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes 2022

*In the absence of unequivocal hyperglycemia, the result should be confirmed by repeat testing.

DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS

Definition

  • Hyperglycemia- blood glucose greater than 200 mg/dL
  • Ketosis - ketones present in blood and/or urine (7.0 mmol/L or higher (serum))
  • Acidosis - pH less than 7.3 and/or bicarbonate less than 15 mmol/L

Diabetic Ketoacidosis Classification Notes:

  • Brain edema can happen with any grade of DKA
  • If the pH and bicarbonate are from different grades consider the most severe grade
  • If pH 7.35-7.30 and bicarbonate 20-15 this patient may enter the DKA any time
  • Severe hypernatremia (corrected Na >150 mEq/L) would also be classified as severe diabetic ketoacidosis. (For each 100mg of serum glucose above 100mg/dl add to measured serum sodium level 1.6 mEq/L)
  • Hyponatremia (corrected Na <130 mEq/L) is a risk factor for brain edema

MANAGEMENT OF DKA:

  • ABC at ER
  • Mild and Moderate cases can be managed in the pediatric ward while severe cases should be admitted to ICU
  • Initial investigations:
    • Blood glucose
    • Urine ketones
    • Venous blood gas
    • Basic blood chemistry (Electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, Magnesium, calcium, phosphorus)
    • CBC
    • Osmolality
  • For newly diagnosed cases
    • HbA1c
    • TSH
    • Islet cell antibodies, insulin auto antibodies and anti-GAD antibodies
    • Ant tissue transglutaminase Ab
  • The patient must be NPO

PROTOCOL NOTES:

IV Fluids
  • Keep the patient NPO to prevent overload and exposure to hypotonic oral solutions
  • The Initial IV bolus is part of the total fluid
  • Bolus can be repeated if the patient still dehydrated
  • Total fluid administered should not exceed 2 times the usual daily maintenance requirement as calculated per weight (use ideal body weight for height for obese children)
  • 5% dehydration (50 ml/kg) can be used for calculation if mild dehydration detected
  • DKA patient dehydration is difficult to assess due to the hypertonic intravascular fluid
  • 0.45% or 0.9% NaCl can be used for rehydration
  • Sodium increases about 1.6 mmol/L for each 100 mg/dL decline in the glucose. If this is not happening better to continue using 0.9 NaCl in IV fluids
  • Maintenance (24 hr) = 100 mL/kg (for the 1st 10 kg) + 50 mL/kg (for the 2nd 10 kg) + 25 mL/kg (for all remaining kg)
  • Give fluids over 36-48 hours if
    • Patient presented with severe DKA, severe hyperglycemia or very high osmolarity
    • corrected sodium level ≥ 150 meg/l
    • ( 1st 24 hr = maintenance + ½ deficit ,2nd 24 hr = maintenance + ½ deficit )
  • Serum glucose shouldn't decrease by more than 80-100 mg/dl/hr
  • If serum glucose decreased > 80-100 mg/dl/hr change the 5% Dextrose to 10 % dextrose
  • If after the 10 % dextrose the serum glucose still decreasing > 80-100 mg/dl/hr start decreasing the insulin infusion rate (see insulin notes)
Insulin
  • Do not give insulin as a bolus
  • For all young patients with newly diagnosed DM start 0.05 IU insulin
  • Tapering insulin dose is very important to prevent brain edema
  • If serum glucose level decrease > 80-100mg/dl/hr decrease rate of insulin infusion
  • If serum glucose level decrease < 50 mg/dl/hr increase insulin infusion rate (check fluid calculation)
  • If insulin dose reached 0.05 IU and still serum glucose decrease > 80-100mg/dl/hr or when serum glucose reached 300 mg /dl add 5% dextrose to the IV (5% dextrose 0.9% NaCl)
  • if serum glucose level decrease > 80-100mg/dl/hr after the 5% dextrose established change it to 10% dextrose
  • Addition of dextrose is more preferred to than decreasing the rates of insulin infusion because this will delay acidosis resolution so dont stop insulin unless its highly indicated
K
  • Make sure there is urine output before establishing K with IV fluids
    • If K > 6 mEq/L = No K initially
    • If K 46 mEq/L = 40 mEq/L
    • If K < 4 mEq/L = 60 mEq/ L
    • If K < 3 mEq/L, give 0.5-1.0 mEq/kg as oral K solution or increase IV K to 80 mEq/L
  • At the start use potassium phosphate or half of the potassium requirement as potassium phosphate
  • Don't use phosphate > 8 hours (can cause hypocalcemic tetany)
  • If potassium phosphate is prescribed as mmol of phosphate and not mEq of potassium
    • Each ml of potassium phosphate contains 4.4 mmol (mEq) potassium and 3 mmol phosphate
    • 40 mEq potassium equal to 27.2 mmol of phosphorus (~9 ml K phosphate)
  • If there is confusion about using potassium phosphate use potassium chloride
  • If K phosphate is not available or not used monitor serum phosphate closely
  • Avoid phosphate if serum Ca2+ low or dropping
pH
  • Initial worsening of acidosis can happen due to
    • potassium is driven into cells in exchange for hydrogen ions
    • chloride effect
    • dilution of bicarbonate
  • Hyperglycemia mostly corrected before pH.
  • pH usually improves in a rate of 0.03 units per hour
  • If pH is not improving check insulin and IV fluids doses and consider infection
  • Bicarbonate not recommended at all by most guidelines
    • It rarely can be considered in cases of extreme acidosis (pH < 6.9 after the initial rehydration)
    • Use it with caution; may cause paradoxical CNS acidosis<< /li>
    • If bicarbonate administration is believed to be necessary,1-2 mmol/kg (added to 0.45% saline) should be provided over 1 to 2 hours.
Recovery mild DKA recover in 10-20 hr
Severe DKA require 30-36 hr

MONITORING OF DKA PATIENT

  • Vital signs:
    • Cardiac monitor
    • Continuous monitoring in ICU
  • Hourly neurologic evaluation
  • ECG if the serum K or Ca are abnormal
  • Laboratory monitoring
    • For the first 4 -6 hours:
      • Every 1 hr blood sugar
      • Every 2 hr VBG, electrolytes, calcium, and phosphate
    • After the 4-6 hours
      • Every 2 hours blood sugar
      • Every 4 hours VBG, electrolytes, calcium, and phosphate
      • Every 6 hours urine for ketones and sugar
    • After 4-6 hours if the patient is improving and electrolytes are stable tests can be done every 6 hours
    • If the initial kidney function is abnormal repeat the test after 6-8 hours

DISCONTINUATION OF INSULIN INFUSION

  • Criteria
    • Venous pH is >7.30
    • Serum HCO3 is >15 mEq/L
    • No vomiting and tolerating oral intake
  • Resolution of urine ketones is not mandatory to stop infusion
  • First subcutaneous dose should be given 30-60 minutes before discontinuation of insulin infusion

Cerebral Edema:

  • Occurs in less than 1% of Pediatric DKA episodes
  • Accounts for 60% to 90% of all DKA deaths
  • 10% to 25% of survivors have permanent neurological injury
  • Occurs most frequently 4-12 hours after therapy initiation
  • May occur before treatment is initiated.

Cerebral Edema Risk Factors:

  • Infants and children < 5 yrs.
  • New-onset diabetes
  • High glucose levels
  • Severe dehydration (elevated BUN/creatinine)
  • Severe acidosis (lower pH, HCO3, pCO2)
  • Treatment with bicarbonate

Bedside Evaluation of Neurologic State of Children with Diabetic Ketoacidosis

Diagnostic Criteria for Cerebral Edema
  • Abnormal motor or verbal response to pain
  • Decorticate or decerebrate posture
  • Cranial nerve palsy (especially III, IV, and VI)
  • Abnormal neurogenic respiratory pattern (eg, grunting, tachypnea, Cheyne-Stokes respirations, apneusis)
Major Criteria Minor criteria
  • Altered mentation or fluctuating level of consciousness
  • Sustained heart rate deceleration
  • Age-inappropriate incontinence
  • Vomiting
  • Headache
  • Lethargy or not being aroused from sleep
  • Diastolic pressure>90 mmHg
  • Age<5 years
  • The presence of any of the first four diagnostic criteria in the table indicates that advanced cerebral edema is present with the likelihood of significant neurologic injury. Prompt intervention is indicated to limit neurologic injury.
  • The presence of two major criteria or one major criterion and two minor criteria represents the presence of early cerebral edema; prompt intervention is indicated to prevent long-standing neurologic injury.
  • Signs that occur before treatment should not be considered in the diagnosis of cerebral edema (eg, vomiting before initial treatment should not be counted)

TREATMENT OF CEREBRAL EDEMA:

  • Reduce the rate of intravenous infusion (2/3 maintenance)
  • Elevate the head of the bed to 30-45 angle
  • Mannitol 0.5 - 1 gram/kg IV over 20 minutes, Mannitol may be repeated if no initial response (improvement in clinical signs) in 2 hours
  • Intubation for impending respiratory failure but avoid aggressive hyperventilation (PCO2 35-45 mmHg)
  • Hypertonic saline (3%) 5-10 ml/kg over 15 minutes can be used as an alternative to Mannitol, or as a second line of treatment if Mannitol fails.
  • CNS imaging studies (non-contrast CT scan) (treatment should not be delayed while waiting for results)
  • Consult pediatric neurologist

ADDITIONAL ORDERS FOR T1DM PATIENTS:

  • Diet
    • Diabetic diet
    • Diet rich in K and phosphate can be ordered especially if serum K is in the lower normal range
  • Education
    • Diabetic education should be offered to the patient and parents or caregiver
    • Diabetic education should be repeated to confirm understanding especially signs of hypoglycemia and correct doses
    • Doctors should participate in education and confirm it was fully understood

Discharge Criteria:

  • All signs of DKA resolved
  • Serum electrolytes, kidney function and urine ketones all are normal
  • The parents well educated about diet, insulin doses and hypoglycemia
  • Blood sugar readings are 140-180 mmol/L (no need to reach optimal control since blood sugar will drop by 10-20% after discharge of most pediatric patients)

Discharge Plan/Instructions:

  • Prescribe the same last doses of basal and regular insulin to the patient
  • Confirm that the patient received the glucose check machine
  • Prescribe three injections of glucagon to be used in cases of hypoglycemia
  • All consumables necessary are offered to the patient.
  • Early appointment within 10 days in the OPD.
  • The family educated how to keep the record of blood sugar (paper or electronic) to be presented in next visit to the OPD.
  • Confirm that the results of thyroid function and celiac screening are released or when they will be released.

TYPE 1 DIABETES WITHOUT KETOACIDOSIS

Start the management from Subcutaneous Insulin Dosing

Clinic follow up for pediatric diabetic patients:

  • Schedule of OPD visit
  • Newly diagnosed T1DM patient first visit should be within 10 days from discharge
  • Recently diagnosed patients are in need for frequent visits (every 2- 3 week) for education and dose adjustment
  • Patents known to have T1DM and well educated can be seen every 2-3 months

Main goals for clinic visit:

  • Check the blood sugar readings and arrange the insulin doses accordingly
  • Education (the Pillar of OPD mission)
    • How the parents/patient can manipulate insulin dose above and below the target blood sugar level
    • Diet , calories, insulin, body weight , blood sugar maintenance , DM complications, hygiene
    • Arrange for Carbohydrate calculation sessions with diabetic educator
    • Sick day management
    • Education about the insulin pump should start only after the patient or parents mastered how to deal with insulin and disease (this usually needs 6-12 month or more after the first diagnosis)
    • Closed Loop insulin pump with continuous glucose check requires extensive education with 24/7 support center
  • Psychological support of the patient and the family
  • Refer the patient to the diabetic educator and to any other specialty needed

Planned Follow up:

  • HbA1c should be monitored every 3 months
  • Nephropathy: annual screening for microalbuminuria After 5 yr. duration in prepubertal children, after 2 yr in pubertal children
  • Retinopathy: annual or biannual ophthalmologic examination After 5 yr. duration in prepubertal children, after 2 yr in pubertal children
  • Thyroid function every 2-3 years
  • Celiac disease screening every 2-3 years
  • Lipids check-up every 3-5 years and more frequent if the patient is obese

Screening

  • Measure fasting blood glucose levels in children
    • Overweight (BMI > 85% for age and gender) and with a family history of type 2 DM in a first- or second-degree relative
    • Signs of insulin resistance (acanthosis nigricans, hypertension, dyslipidemia, polycystic ovarian disease)
  • Begin at age 10 years or onset of puberty (whichever occurs first), and repeat every 2 years
  • HbA1c may be used as a screening tool:
    • HbA1c =5.7%6.4% indicates an increased risk of future diabetes
    • HbA1c =6.0%6.5% is abnormal and indicates the need for further testing (OGTT, fasting plasma glucose);
    • HbA1c = >6.5% is diagnostic of diabetes.

Pediatric Fever without focus

DEFINITION

  • Normal Temperature range (36.6- 37.9ᵒC rectally)
  • Fever is defined as a rectal temperature ≥38ᵒC.
  • Fever without focus: Fever of acute onset, with duration of <1 week and cause could not been identified
  • Fever of Unknown Origin: a temperature higher than 38.0ᵒC (100.4F) that lasts longer than at least 8 days without a clear source.
  • Sustained fever: persistent and does not vary by more than 0.5ᵒC/day
  • Remittent fever: persistent and varies by more than 0.5ᵒC/day
  • Relapsing fever: febrile periods separated by intervals of normal temperature

AETIOLOGY

  • The causes of fever can be organized into 4 main categories:
    • Infectious
    • Inflammatory
    • Neoplastic
    • Miscellaneous.
  • The most common causes of acute fever:
    • Self-limited viral infections (common cold, gastroenteritis)
    • uncomplicated bacterial infections (otitis media, pharyngitis, sinusitis)

EVALUATION

  • Thorough history
  • Physical examination: complete, with focus on localizing symptoms.
    • General: Ill appearance, lethargy, tachycardia are typically present among children with severe infection
    • Localized: Don't miss
      • Deep neck infections (neck pain, drooling) like retropharyngeal abscess and peritonsillar abscess
      • Joint examination: septic arthritis, osteomyelitis
  • Laboratory studies on a case-by-case basis.

FEVER MANAGEMENT

  • Hydration (The first step to replace fluids loss)
  • Bathing or sponging (with lukewarm water): Do not use cold water, ice baths, or alcohol.
  • Educate the family about fever to minimize the fever phobia
  • The response of fever to antipyretic therapy should not be used as a prognostic factor for determining whether the child has a less serious illness.
  • Medications:
    • Paracetamol (10-15 mg/kg/dose every 4 hr).
    • Ibuprofen in children older than 6 months (dose of 5-10 mg/kg/dose every 8 hrs)

FEVER WITHOUT FOCUS SOURCE

  • Evaluation and Management:Etiology and evaluation of fever without localizing signs depends on the child age:
    • Neonates (<28 days)
    • Infants (1 to 3 months)
    • Children (>3 months to 3 years)
  • Low-risk criteria for febrile infants:(Clinical criteria)
    • Previously healthy
    • Nontoxic clinical appearance
    • Normal physical examination (except otitis media)
    • Meningeal signs negative
  • Laboratory criteria
    • WBC count (5-15) X 103/μL (< 1500 bands/μL)
    • Urine: negative leukocyte esterase, nitrite and <10 WBC/HPF
    • Stool studies if diarrhoea (no RBC and <5 WBC/HPF)
    • Chest radiograph without infiltrate
    • CSF cell count (<5 WBC/μL) and negative Gram stain

EMPIRICAL ANTIBIOTIC SELECTION

  • Age (0-2) months: Ampicillin + (cefotaxime or gentamicin)
  • Age (2-36) months: third generation cephalosporin

COMMENTS:

  • Meningitis
    • Approximately 35% of infants with bacterial meningitis do not have a positive blood culture
    • Young febrile infants with bronchiolitis may not require LP
    • If urine analysis is abnormal, its extremely rare for the patient to have meningitis (infants >28 days <0.5%. For neonates 0-28 days, 12%.)
    • Sterile CSF pleocytosis has been reported in infants with UTI.
    • for traumatic LP, adjustment of CSF WBC count does not improve the accuracy of diagnosis of meningitis
  • UTI
    • The most common bacterial cause for fever without localizing signs in children aged 3-36 months is a urinary tract infection (UTI).
    • Children 2-24 months with fever without focus are having 5-10% chance to have UTI
  • Immunosuppressed children and those with central venous catheter should undergo diagnostic testing and receive prompt antimicrobial therapy due to their higher risk of invasive bacterial infection
  • Vaccinated children are having very low risk of occult bacteremia so the routine performance of blood culture is not indicated in most of them
  • CXR is indicated for children with hypoxemia or significant respiratory distress from suspected infectious cause
  • Infants with history of preterm birth or chronic medical conditions are having high risk of having bacterial infections
  • If pathogenic bacteria are present, 96% of blood culture results become positive by 36 hours and 91% are positive by 24 hours.
  • C‐reactive protein (CRP) and procalcitonin (PCT):
    • CRP can often be elevated in viral infection
    • CRP does not rise as acutely as PCT levels values are associated
    • PCT superiority in bacterial infection diagnosis is controversial
  • RSV screening
    • Infants 29-90 days with positive RSV screening and fever ≤38.5 need only observation and bronchiolitis management.
    • Infants 29-90 days with positive RSV screening and fever >38.5, CBC and urine analysis need to be evaluated.

DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • Infants should remain in the hospital receiving antimicrobial therapy until bacterial cultures were negative for 36-48 hr or even longer
  • Afebrile patient
  • Normal oral intake

DISCHARGE PLAN/INSTRUCTIONS

  • Home fever management education
  • Follow-up in the Pediatric outpatient clinic

Pediatric Anaphylaxis

Definition:

Rapid-onset (minutes to hours) usually immunoglobulin E (IgE)-mediated systemic allergic reaction involving multiple organ systems, including two or more of the following:

  • Cutaneous/mucosal (80% to 90%): flushing, urticaria, pruritis, angioedema
  • Respiratory (70%): laryngeal edema, bronchospasm, dyspnea, wheezing, stridor, hypoxemia
  • Gastrointestinal (45%): vomiting, diarrhea, nausea, crampy abdominal pain
  • Circulatory (45%): tachycardia, hypotension, syncope

Management:

  • Stop exposure to precipitating antigens.
  • While performing A-B-Cs, immediately give intramuscular (IM) epinephrine into the mid-anterolateral thigh.
    • For a child, administer 01 mg/kg of 1 mg/mL solution up to a max dose of 1 mg/dose.
    • For adult-sized patients, first administer 2 to 0.5 mg of 1 mg/mL solution, increasing as necessary up to a max single dose of 1 mg.
  • Autoinjector dosing:
    • 5 to <15 kg use 0.1 mg,
    • 15 to <30 kg use 0.15 mg,
    • ≥30 kg use 0.3 mg.
  • Repeat dosing every 5 to 15 minutes as needed.
  • Provide oxygen and ventilatory assistance. Consider early endotracheal intubation.
  • Obtain IV access.
  • For management of shock, resuscitate with 20 mL/ kg isotonic crystalloid fluid boluses and vasoactive agents as needed.
  • Place the patient in Trendelenburg position (head 30 degrees below feet).
  • Consider adjuvant pharmacologic agents:
    • Histamine receptor antagonist: Diphenhydramine (H1-antagonism) and famotidine (H2-antagonism)
    • Corticosteroid: Methylprednisolone or dexamethasone
    • Inhaled β2 agonist: Albuterol
  • Symptoms may recur (“biphasic anaphylaxis”) up to 72 hours after initial recovery.
    • Observe for a minimum of 4 to 10 hours for late-phase symptoms.
  • Discharge with an epinephrine autoinjector and an anaphylaxis action plan.

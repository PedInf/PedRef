Acute Bronchiolitis

DEFINITION

An acute inflammation leading to edema, necrosis of epithelial cells lining of small airways, and increased mucus production caused mainly by viruses

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

Typically begin with rhinitis and cough, which may progress to tachypnea, wheezing, rales, use of accessory muscles, and/or nasal flaring

CAUSES

  1. Viruses mainly respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) (50-70% of cases)
  2. Highest incidence between December and March (North America)
  3. 90% of children are infected with RSV in the first 2 years of life
  4. 40% experience lower respiratory tract infection during the initial infection
  5. Infection with RSV does not grant permanent or long-term immunity

RISK FACTORS FOR SEVERE DISEASE

  1. Age less than 12 weeks
  2. History of prematurity
  3. Underlying cardiopulmonary disease, or immunodeficiency

INVESTIGATIONS

  • Outpatient
    • In most outpatient cases no need for investigations
  • Inpatients
    • CBC: WBC count and differential help if bacterial infection is suspected
    • Electrolytes and kidney function: help in dehydration management and if there is hyponatremia either iatrogenic or due to excessive releases of antidiuretic hormone
    • CXR: not indicated in all cases (Strength: Strong, Quality: B)

TREATMENT

Recommended

  • Fluids (IV or NG)
    • Initial bolus if there is dehydration
    • Full maintenance if the patient NPO due to distress and tachypnea
    • Supplementary fluids if the patient not taking enough oral intake
  • Oxygen
    • To keep saturation above 90%
    • In moderate to severe cases use high flow nasal canula
    • Adjust flow rate to keep saturation 90-92%
    • Use flow of 1-2 Liter/kg/minute with a maximum of 12L/minute
  • Nasal suctioning
    • Provides relief of distress or cyanosis
    • most beneficial before feeding and in response to copious secretions
    • No evidence exists to support deep suctioning of the lower pharynx.
    • excessive suction can be associated with nasal edema and lead to additional obstruction

Not to be Used

  • Salbutamol (Strength: Strong, Quality: B)
    • Should not be used in patients with isolated acute bronchiolitis.
    • One trial of salbutamol can be used
    • If the patient showed signs of improvement (decrease respiratory rate, improved saturation, decreased wheezes) it can be continued
    • Bronchodilators even if they cause the above improvements, they will not affect the disease course, length of stay, or oxygen need
    • If the patient didn't improve after the first trial. salbutamol should be stopped
  • Nebulized epinephrine should not be used (Strength: Strong, Quality: B)
  • Nebulized hypertonic saline
    • Should not be administered in the emergency department (Strength: Moderate, Quality: B)
    • Its effectiveness after admission is inconsistent
    • It can be used in cases of atelectasis
    • It can cause severe cough in some patients
  • Chest Physiotherapy should not be used routinely (atelectasis excluded)
  • Antibiotics should not be used unless there is a concomitant bacterial infection, or a strong suspicion of one

CONTRAINDICATED

  • Clinicians should not administer systemic corticosteroids to infants with a diagnosis of acute bronchiolitis in any setting (Strength: Strong, Quality: A)
  • Sedatives (antihistamines) can lead to sedation and more hypoxia
  • Nasal decongestant drops no evidence supports the use of such medications

INFECTION CONTROL

  • Disinfect hands before and after direct contact with patient or his/her objects
  • Use alcohol-based rubs

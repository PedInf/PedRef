Community Acquired Pneumonia

DEFINITION

Pneumonia: An inflammation of the lung parenchyma caused by microorganisms,
noninfectious causes include aspiration (of food or gastric acid, foreign bodies, hydrocarbons, and lipoid substances), hypersensitivity reactions, and drug- or radiation-induced pneumonitis

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP): An acute pulmonary infection in a previously healthy individual acquired in the community

MOST COMMON MICROORGANISMS OF CAP

0-3 mo Group B streptococcus
Gram-negative bacteria
Streptococcus pneumonia
Viruses 15-30 %

CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

  • Fever, cough, tachypnea, increased work of breathing (e.g., nasal flaring in infants, retractions) and hypoxia
  • Tachypnea is more sensitive at age < 5 years but less sensitive and specific as age increases
  • Myalgia, headache, photophobia
  • Hoarseness (Chlamydia pneumonia) and wheezing a child older than 5 years (Mycoplasma or Chlamydia) suggest atypical pneumonia
  • Upper lobe pneumonias may present with a clinical picture suggestive of meningitis
  • Lower lobe pneumonias may present as vague abdominal pain mimicking appendicitis
  • It is virtually impossible to clinically differentiate bacterial from viral pneumonia except that bacterial pneumonia might have a more abrupt and severe onset after days of symptoms of an upper respiratory tract infection.

INVESTIGATIONS

Chest radiographs

  • Patients 4 years and above posteroanterior upright position
  • Patients less than 4 years supine anteroposterior position
  • Lateral view is preferred if lobar pneumonia is present
  • Decubitus position for effusion or empyema (Ultrasound my give better information)
  • Follow-up chest radiographs not routinely indicated
  • Follow-up radiographs are indicated in
    • Complicated pneumonias that are clinically unstable
    • Patients receiving adequate antibiotic coverage for 48-72 hours with poor clinical improvement or worsening
    • Recurrent pneumonias that involve the same lobe to rule out a suspected anomaly, chest mass, or foreign body

Ultrasonography

  • Specificity of ultrasonography reach to 97% in children with consolidation greater than 1 cm by chest radiographs
  • Effusion or empyema

Blood investigations

  • Complete blood cell count with differential:(Does not allow differentiation among bacterial, atypical, or viral)
  • Acute phase reactants (CRP and serum procalcitonin): (Should not be routinely measured)
  • Serum electrolytes
    • Assess dehydration and to rule out hyponatremia secondary to syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion.
    • Not routinely indicated in the outpatient setting

Blood cultures

  • Not routinely indicated children who have nontoxic effects and fully immunized
  • Yield only positive in 10-12% of children
  • In patients with parapneumonic effusion or empyema the yield increases to 30-40%.
  • Follow-up blood cultures are not necessary in patients with clear improvement

Laboratory Findings in Empyema

  • pH <7.1
  • Glucose <40 mg/dL
  • Lactate dehydrogenase >1000 IU/mL

HOSPITALIZATION INDICATIONS

  • Hypoxia (oxygen saturations < 90-92%)
  • Infants <3-6 months with suspected bacterial infection
  • Tachypnea:
    • Infants <12 months of age: respiratory rate >70 breaths/min
    • Children: respiratory rate >50 breaths/min
  • Respiratory distress: apnea, grunting, difficulty breathing, and poor feeding
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Inability to maintain hydration or oral intake
  • Capillary refill time >2 seconds
  • Infants and children with toxic appearance or suspected or confirmed to have infection with a virulent organism (CA-MRSA or group A Streptococcus)
  • Underlying conditions comorbidities that:
    • May predispose patients to a more serious course (e.g., cardiopulmonary disease, genetic syndromes, neurocognitive disorders)
    • May be worsened by pneumonia (e.g., metabolic disorder)
    • May adversely affect response to treatment (e.g., immunocompromised host, sickle cell disease)
  • Complications (e.g., effusion/empyema)
  • Failure of outpatient therapy (48-72 hours with no response)
  • Caretaker unable to provide appropriate observation or to comply with prescribed home therapy

INDICATIONS FOR ICU ADMISSION

  • Severe respiratory distress or impending respiratory failure requiring
    • Intubation and mechanical ventilation
    • Positive pressure ventilation
  • Recurrent apnea or slow irregular respirations
  • Cardiopulmonary monitoring due to cardiovascular compromise secondary to:
    • Sustained tachycardia
    • Inadequate blood pressure
  • Requires pharmacologic support of blood pressure or perfusion
  • Altered mental status due to hypercarbia or hypoxemia
  • Pediatric Early Warning Score >6

TREATMENT

Supportive care

  • Antipyretics
  • Suctioning
  • Hydration
  • Mucolytics and cough suppressants have no role in the treatment of pneumonia
  • Zinc supplementation (in case of recurrent severe pneumonia in children < 5 years with evidence of zinc deficiency)
  • Chest physiotherapy: There is no evidence that its beneficial in the management of pneumonia or leads to a decrease in the length of stay or a change in the outcome

Outpatient Antibiotics

  • Amoxicillin or amoxicillin-clavulanate the most appropriate first-line antimicrobial agent because S pneumonia remains the most commonly implicated pathogen
  • High doses (90-100 mg/kg daily) may overcome resistance
  • Macrolide (azithromycin or clarithromycin) in school-aged children and teens with a clinical picture compatible with atypical CAP

Inpatient Antibiotics

  • Based on antibiogram of the hospital mainly regarding S pneumonia
  • Amoxicillin (80-90 mg/kg/24 hr)
  • Cefuroxime axetil
  • Amoxicillin/clavulanate
  • Third-generation cephalosporin (cefotaxime at 200 mg/kg daily) used in
    • Infants and children who are not fully immunized
    • Penicillin-resistant pneumococcal strains
    • Infants and children with severe life-threatening infections
    • Pneumonia complications, such as empyema.
  • Macrolides (Oral or parenteral) in patients with suspected Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • Vancomycin, linezolid, or clindamycin if clinical or radiographic findings suggestive of Staph aureus
  • Children proved to be allergic to B-lactams, you can use carbapenems (meropenem, 20-40 mg/kg per dose every 8 hours), or Fluoroquinolones (eg, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin)
  • Anaerobes coverage in cases of lung abscess or aspiration pneumonia

Duration of treatment

  • Uncomplicated total duration 7-10 days (parenteral and oral therapy)
  • Two weeks or more in complicated cases
  • If No clinical improvement or deterioration of the clinical picture after adequate antibiotic coverage for 48-72 hours do further investigation to rule out alternative diagnosis (foreign body), antibiotic resistance, or complicated pneumonia.

DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • Clinical improvement in the level of activity and appetite
  • Decreased fever for at least 12- 24 hours.
  • Sustained pulse oximetry measurements greater than 90% in room air for at least 12 to 24 hours.
  • Stable and/or baseline mental and cardiorespiratory status
  • Ability to tolerate their home anti-infective regimen and the caretaker at home has the ability to administer therapy
  • Ability to maintain adequate fluid and nutrition orally
  • Chest tube must have been discontinued 12-24 hours before discharge with no clinical evidence of deterioration

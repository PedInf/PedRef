IV Fluids in Pediatric Patients

INTRODUCTION

Dehydration is a condition that can occur with excess loss of water and other body fluids.
Dehydration results from decreased intake, increased output (renal, gastrointestinal or insensible losses), a shift of fluids (ascitic, effusions) and capillary leak of fluids (burns and sepsis).

Clinical assessment of dehydration is only an estimate that is why the patient must be continually reevaluated during therapy.
Usually underestimated in hypernatremic dehydration because the movement of water from the intracellular space to the extracellular space helps preserve the intravascular volume.

INVESTIGATIONS

  • Types:
    • Investigations for the primary etiology
    • Investigations for dehydration
    • Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride, bicarbonate)
    • Kidney function (BUN, creatinine)
    • Urine analysis (ketones, urine specific gravity)
    • Serum pH
    • Blood glucose level
    • ECG monitor for any cardiac arrhythmias from electrolyte disturbance.
  • Repeat:
    • Investigations for dehydration can be repeated every 4-6 hours in severe dehydration and more sometimes according to the clinical condition

MANAGEMENT

  • ACTION PLAN
    • Mild and moderate dehydration can be managed at ER and discharged unless the underlying aetiology is the indication of admission
    • If the patient tolerate oral intake, Oral rehydration solutions are less risky than IV solutions
    • All cases with severe dehydration should be admitted

Initial resuscitation and rehydration phase

  • Needed mainly in moderate to severe dehydration and mandatory in shock
  • 20 mL/kg of the isotonic fluid (Normal saline (NS) or Ringer lactate (RL)) over 20 minutes.
  • In a child with a known or probable metabolic alkalosis (the child with isolated vomiting), RL should not be used because the lactate would worsen the alkalosis.
  • If child remains shocked after the first bolus, immediately give another rapid intravenous infusion of 20 ml/kg of 0.9% sodium chloride.
  • Can be repeated several times, but if the child remains shocked after second rapid intravenous infusion Consider other causes of shock other than dehydration.
  • Colloids (albumin, plasma) rarely used unless there is severe anemia or severe hypoalbuminemia
  • The initial resuscitation and rehydration phase is complete when the child has an adequate intravascular volume.
  • Typically, the child shows clinical improvement, including a lower heart rate, improvement of the conscious level, normalization of blood pressure, improved perfusion, return of peripheral pulses, return of normal skin colour and warmth, better urine output (newborn up to 1 year: 2 ml/kg/hour, toddlers 1.5 ml/kg/hour, older children 1 ml/kg/hour).
  • Plan the fluid therapy for the next 24 hours.

Duration of fluid therapy in dehydration:

    • Isonatremic or hyponatremic dehydration, the entire fluid deficit is corrected over 24 hrs.
    • Hypernatremic dehydration require slower approach

DAILY FLUID REQUIREMENT

Daily Fluid Requirement=(Maintenance +Deficit)-Initial Boluses

  • Maintenance:
    • 100 mL/kg (for the 1st 10 kg) + 50 mL/kg (for the 2nd 10 kg) + 20 mL/kg (for all remaining kg)
    • 4 mL/kg/hr (for the 1st 10 kg) + 2 mL/kg/hr (for the 2nd 10 kg) + 1 mL/kg/hr (for all remaining kg)
    • The maximum total fluid per day as maintenance is normally 2,400 mL or 100 mL/hr
  • Deficit:
    • Deficit (ml) = body weight (kg) X dehydration% X 10.
  • On-going losses:
    • Equal to volume lost (urine, stool, drain)
    • Replace losses mL/mL every 1-6 hr
  • Fluid type
    • 5% dextrose NS + 20 mEq/L KCl

KCl started after the patient voided and calculated according to serum potassium

Maintenance fluids usually contain D5, which provides 17 calories/ 100 mL and nearly 20% of the daily caloric needs.
This level is enough to prevent ketone production and helps minimize protein degradation, but the child will lose weight on this regimen.

MONITORING FLUID THERAPY

The formulation of a plan for correcting a childs dehydration is only the beginning of management.
All calculations in fluid therapy are only approximations.
This statement is especially true for the assessment of percentage dehydration.
It is equally important to monitor the patient during treatment and to modify therapy on the basis of the clinical situation

  • Vital signs:
    • Pulse
    • Blood pressure
  • Intake and output:
    • Fluid balance
    • Urine output
    • Physical examination:
      • Weight
      • Clinical signs of depletion or overload
    • Electrolytes
  • Notes:
    • Measurement of serum electrolyte levels at least daily for any child who is receiving only intravenous rehydration as such a child is at risk for sodium, potassium and acid-base disorders.
  • Adjusting fluid therapy for altered renal output:
    • Oliguria/Anuria:
      • Start patient on replacement of insensible fluid losses
      • Replace urine output mL/mL with half-normal saline
    • Polyuria:
      • Start patient on replacement of insensible fluid losses
      • Measure urine electrolytes
      • Replace urine output mL/mL with solution based on measured urine electrolytes
  • Notes:
    • Insensible losses represent approximately 1/3 of total maintenance water (40% in infants and 25% in adolescents and adults)
    • Insensible losses are more with fever and tachypnea

HYPONATREMIC DEHYDRATION

  • Rule out Factitious Hyponatremia:
    • Hyperlipidaemia: Na decreased by 0.002 lipid (mg/dL)
    • Hyperproteinaemia: Na decreased by 0.25 [protein (g/dL) âˆ’ 8]
    • Hyperglycemia: Na decreased 1.6 mEq/L for each 100 mg/dL rise in glucose
  • Rehydration phase:
    • 20 ml/kg (NS or RL)
  • Maintenance phase:
    • In most patients the same principles of dehydration management are adequate
    • Rapid (>12 mEq/L/24 hr or > 18 mEq/L/48 hr) or overcorrection in the serum sodium concentration (>135 mEq/L) is associated with an increased risk of central pontine myelinolysis
    • Monitor serum sodium
    • Desmopressin is a potential option if the serum sodium level is increasing too rapidly
  • Symptomatic Hyponatremia:
    • Patients with seizures as a result of hyponatremia need to receive an acute infusion of hypertonic (3%) saline to increase the serum sodium concentration rapidly
    • Each mL/kg of 3% sodium chloride increases the serum sodium by approximately 1 mEq/L.
    • Dose: 4-6 mL/kg of 3% sodium chloride given as STAT dose

HYPERNATREMIC DEHYDRATION

Usually, degree of Hypernatremic dehydration is under estimated due to doughy skin Serum sodium should not decrease by >10 mEq/L every 24 hr Serum sodium values greater than 160 mEq/L require immediate attention Quick correction can be done only if hypernatremia is acute (iatrogenic), but this needs a lot of care In most cases the following steps are enough
  • Rehydration phase:
    • The initial resuscitation of hypernatremic dehydration requires restoration of the intravascular volume with NS 20 ml/kg.
      RL should not be used because it is more hypotonic than NS and may cause too rapid a decrease in the serum sodium concentration, especially if multiple fluid boluses are necessary.
  • Maintenance phase:
    • Determine time for correction on basis of initial sodium concentration:
      • [Na] 145-157 mEq/L: 24 hr
      • [Na] 158-170 mEq/L: 48 hr
      • [Na] 171-183 mEq/L: 72 hr
      • [Na] 184-196 mEq/L: 84 hr
    • Typical fluid: Dextrose 5%- 0.45% Saline (with 20 mEq/L KCl unless contraindicated)
    • Typical rate: 1.25-1.5 times maintenance
  • Follow serum sodium concentration
    • Initially start by 6 hourly then 12 hourly then daily
    • Sodium decreases too rapidly:
      • Increase sodium concentration of intravenous fluid
      • Decrease rate of intravenous fluid
    • Sodium decreases too slowly:
      • Decrease sodium concentration of intravenous fluid
      • Increase rate of intravenous fluid

UNCONTROLLED HYPERNATREMIA

Patients with severe hypernatremia or difficult to control the drop in serum Sodium are in need for more care
  • Free water deficit:
    • Its the total volume of free water needed to restore sodium concentration
    • Water deficient (L) = body weight (kg) X0.6X (1-145/[serum Na])
    • This volume divided based on sodium level in maintenance phase
  • Two IV lines:
    • Use 2 intravenous solutions (D5,1/4 NS and D5 NS, both with the same concentration of potassium) at the bedside can facilitate this approach by allowing for rapid adjustments of the rates of the 2 fluids.
  • Special considerations:
    • Infants due to high insensible water losses, may need to receive D5 0.2NS
    • Diabetes insipidus, usually needs a more hypotonic fluid than a child with depletion of both sodium and water due to diarrhoea
    • In Diabetes insipidus give desmopressin as part of the treatment
    • If a child has seizures as a result of brain edema secondary to rapid correction, administration of hypotonic fluid should be stopped. An infusion of 3% saline can acutely increase the serum sodium; an infusion of 4 mL/kg often results in resolution of the symptoms reversing the cerebral edema.
    • Hyperglycemia from hypernatremia is not usually a problem and is not treated with insulin because the acute decrease in glucose may precipitate cerebral edema by lowering plasma osmolality. Rarely, the glucose concentration of intravenous fluids must be reduced (from D5W to D2.5W).

DEHYDRATION DUE TO GASTROENTERITIS:

  • Management of dehydration is the same like general dehydration management
  • If there is Hypo or Hypernatremia follow the above-mentioned management

  • Additional considerations:
    • Stool analysis for viral and parasitic causative agents
    • Stool culture for all bloody diarrhea or febrile patients
    • Antibiotics not indicated in most cases of gastroenteritis and may prolong carrier stage and relapses in non-typhoid Salmonella also may precipitate hemolytic uremic syndrome in Shiga toxin producing Escherichia coli
    • Antibiotics (ceftriaxone 75 mg/kg) for all cases with suspected Shigella dysentery (highly febrile and toxic patients)
    • Antimotility agents (loperamide) are contraindicated in children with dysentery and probably have no role in the management of acute watery diarrhea in otherwise healthy children

ANTIEMETIC

  • Ondansetron is an effective antiemetic agent (0.2 mg/kg) as a single dose
  • Phenothiazines are of little value and are associated with potentially serious side effects (lethargy, dystonia, malignant hyperpyrexia)
  • Domperidone associated with cardiac side effects (not approved by FDA)
  • Metoclopramide has extrapyramidal reactions reported up to 25% of children

Notes

  • Zinc level for all patients with prolonged or recurrent diarrhea, if zinc level is low give zinc 10 mg/day for infants < 6 mo of age and 20 mg/day for those > 6 mo for 10-14 days
  • Metabolic screening patients with recurrent diarrhea with metabolic acidosis resistant to management
  • Consider Congenital adrenal hyperplasia in children with persistent hyponatremia and hyperkalemia

RED FLAGS IN TREATMENT OF DEHYDRATION:

  • Developing haematuria: raises the suspicion of development of renal vein thrombosis, which can be caused by severe dehydration, investigations will reveal thrombocytopenia and haematuria.
  • Developing seizures and disturbed conscious levels after starting IV fluids:
    • Could be caused by cerebral edema as a result of rapid correction of hypernatremia.
    • Could be caused by rapid correction of severe hyponatremia with serum sodium levels 120 mEq/L or less for more than 48 hours.
  • Developing seizure and disturbed conscious level before starting IV fluids:
    • Could be caused by hyponatremia, hypokalemia, hyperkalemia or hypoglycemia which should to be corrected immediately.

DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • The patient to be discharged after complete correction of dehydration
  • Correction of serum electrolyte levels
  • Oral intake improved

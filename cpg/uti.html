Pediatric Urinary Tract Infection

DEFINITION

  • Symptomatic bacteriuria: Upper or lower urinary tract symptoms and urine culture with significant bacterial colony counts
  • Urethritis: Inflammation of the urethral mucosa, with symptoms of Dysuria, frequency, secondary enuresis, pyuria, and low urine colony counts (<103) (rule out vaginitis in sexually active patients).
  • Acute cystitis: Inflammation of bladder mucosa and symptoms of lower tract infections: urgency, dysuria, frequency, and hematuria.
  • Parenchyma infection/acute pyelonephritis: Inflammation of renal parenchyma and symptoms of upper tract infection: high fever (temperature >39C), abdominal or flank pain, and other systemic symptoms (eg, vomiting).
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria: Urine culture with significant bacterial colony count in an asymptomatic patient.
  • Complicated bacteriuria: Urine culture with significant bacterial colony count and associated urologic abnormalities (hydroureter, hydronephrosis, and vesicoureteral reflux).

CLINICAL SYMPTOMS AND SIGNS

  • 0-3 months symptoms nonspecific: fever, hypothermia, vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, difficulty feeding, malodorous urine, irritability, hematuria, or failure to thrive.
  • 3-24 months more specific symptoms: cloudy or malodorous urine, frequency, or hematuria
  • 2-6 years of age-specific symptoms: abdominal pain, suprapubic pain, costovertebral angle pain, dysuria, urgency, or secondary enuresis in a previously toilet-trained child.
  • Temperature > 38.5C helps to differentiate acute pyelonephritis from lower tract UTIs.
  • Fever can be the only symptom.

DIAGNOSIS

  • Urine culture
    • Gold standard for UTI diagnosis
    • Obtained by suprapubic aspiration (SPA), urethral catheterization, a clean catch midstream specimen, or urine bag collection (least preferable).
    • 88% false positive in bag sample.
    • Urine specimen is obtained for both culture and urinalysis before an antimicrobial agent is administered.
    • To establish the diagnosis of UTI, clinicians should require both
      • Urinalysis results that suggest infection (pyuria and/or bacteriuria)
      • 50,000 colony-forming units (CFUs) per mL of a uropathogen cultured from a urine specimen obtained through catheterization or SPA.
    • Suprapubic Aspiration: Any growth of gram-negative bacilli.
  •  Nitrite
    • Nitrate converted to nitrite by gram negative bacteria
    • 37% sensitive and 100% specific
    • Positive predictive value and negative predictive value of 90% and 100%
  •  Leukocyte esterase
    • Sensitivity and specificity is 73%
    • Positive predictive value is 34%, and the negative predictive value is 95%.
  •  Pyuria
    • Not adequate to diagnose a UTI and is not a substitute for a urine culture.
    • May be related to vaginal secretions, dehydration, glomerulonephritis, tuberculosis, or interstitial nephritis and should be differentiated from a UTI.
  •  Microbiology
    • Escherichia coli 70% of infections.

Management and Treatment

Antibiotics

  • Initiating treatment orally or parenterally is equally efficacious
  • Choice of route of administration on practical considerations
  • The choice of agent on local antimicrobial sensitivity patterns
  • Adjust the choice according to sensitivity testing of the isolated uropathogen.
  • The clinician should choose 7-14 days as the duration of antimicrobial therapy
  • When there is prolonged fever or renal abscess, treating for up to 14 days may be reasonable.
  • In most cases with appropriate treatment, the cultures become sterile by 48 hours

ANTIBIOTIC TYPES

  • Pyelonephritis
    • Ceftriaxone (50-75 mg/kg/24 hr not to exceed 2 g)
    • Cefotaxime (100 mg/kg/24 hr)
    • ampicillin (100 mg/kg/24 hr) with an aminoglycoside such as gentamicin (3-5 mg/kg/24 hr in 1-3 divided doses)
  • Cystitis
    • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) a 3-5-day
    • Nitrofurantoin (5-7 mg/kg/24 hr in 3-4 divided doses)

HOSPITALIZATION INDICATIONS

  • 3 Months and younger should be hospitalized to receive intravenous fluids and antibiotics.
  • Clinical urosepsis (eg, toxic appearance, hypotension, poor capillary refill)
  • Dehydration or the inability to take oral fluids.
  • Vomiting or inability to tolerate oral medications
  • Lack of adequate outpatient follow-up (eg, no telephone, live far from hospital, etc.)
  • Failure to respond to outpatient therapy
  • Chronic diseases such as sickle cell disease, diabetes mellitus, immunocompromised, cystic fibrosis, urinary tract abnormalities, or other serious conditions when the infection could compromise the chronic illness.

ULTRASOUND

  • Febrile infants with UTIs should undergo renal and bladder ultrasonography (RBUS).
  • Renal Ultrasonography is Safe and fast.
  • Not a good imaging for diagnosing VUR in low-grade VUR.
  • Not sensitive for diagnosing renal scars or acute pyelonephritis.
  • The timing of RBUS is usually during hospitalization for inpatients and within 2-4 weeks for outpatients.

VCUG

  • The gold standard for diagnosing VUR
  • VCUG should not be performed routinely after the first febrile UTI
  • VCUG is indicated if
    • RBUS reveals hydronephrosis, scarring, or other findings that would suggest either high-grade VUR or obstructive uropathy.
    • Other atypical or complex clinical circumstances.
  • VCUG with further evaluation should be conducted if there is a recurrence of febrile UTI.

FOLLOW-UP

After confirmation of UTI, the clinician should instruct parents or guardians to seek prompt medical evaluation (ideally within 48 hours) for future febrile illnesses, to ensure that recurrent infections can be detected and treated promptly rather than on regularly repeated urine cultures after treatment.

VESICOURETERAL REFLUX

  • Resolution
  • All grades of primary VUR have the potential for spontaneous resolution over a period of time.
  • Resolution rate in Children younger than 5 years of age
    • Grade I 82%
    • Grade II 80%
    • Grade III 46%
    • Grade IV 30%
    • Grade V 13%

VESICOURETERAL REFLUX TREATMENT

  • No statistically significant benefit could be demonstrated for prophylaxis in those without VUR or those with grades I to IV VUR 
  • Long-term antimicrobial prophylaxis can protect from repeated UTIs in grade "IV" and "V" but it increases the risk of resistant bacteria 
  • Antimicrobial agents
    • trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMZ) (avoided in neonates)
    • trimethoprim alone
    • nitrofurantoin
    • cephalexin
    • Ampicillin and amoxicillin
      • less effective (E coli resistance)
      • Used in the first 2 postnatal months

DURATION

  • Depend on the age of the patient, the severity of the VUR, the frequency of UTI, patient tolerance, compliance with antimicrobial prophylaxis, presence and severity of renal scarring, and duration of follow-up. 
  • Medical management is appropriate for all grades of primary VUR, particularly in younger children 
  • Surgical management is reserved for patients who fail medical management by having breakthrough UTIs or persistent VUR, particularly with evidence of progressive renal injury. 
  • Medical management must include treatment of constipation and voiding dysfunction if present.

Follow-up VCUG

  • Not well defined
  • Yearly studies
  • Every 2 years in children who have mild VUR (grades I and II)
  • Every 3 years in children who have moderate/severe VUR (grades III or higher)

NEUROGENIC BLADDER

  • Urine may be colonized with gram-negative bacteria
  • There is no evidence that chronic bacteriuria in patients who have had neurogenic bladder is associated with renal damage
  • Only symptomatic UTIs should be treated 
  • Attempts to sterilize the urine for prolonged periods with prophylactic or active treatment
    • Usually fail
    • This leads to the emergence of highly resistant strains
    • Colonization of more
  • Symptoms suggestive of UTI
    • Fever
    • Abdominal or loin pain
    • Dysuria (minimal in the neurogenic bladder)
    • Change in color or odor of urine
  • Factors that help in decreasing UTI
    • Intermittent catheterization
    • Constipation treatment
    • Proper hygiene
    • Anticholinergic

NEONATES WITH DILATED RENAL PELVIS

  • All newborns with a history of prenatal dilated pelvis should have a confirmatory ultrasound postnatally.
  • If no dilation is found on the initial sonogram, repeat the study every three months till resolved if it didn't resolve spacing of scans can be done (6 months to 1 year).
  • The majority of cases with mild or moderate postnatal hydronephrosis resolve by 18 months of age
  • If there is unilateral or bilateral hydronephrosis follow the below recommendations.

UNILATERAL FETAL HYDRONEPHROSIS

  • Fetal hydronephrosis (prenatal RPD >10 mm on an antenatal ultrasound performed in the third trimester) with a normal-appearing contralateral kidney, ultrasonography should be performed when the infant returns to birth weight usually within the first two to four weeks of life
    • <10 mm Normal or mild hydronephrosis. Grade 1.
    • 10 to 15 mm Moderate hydronephrosis. Grade 2.
    • >15 mm Severe hydronephrosis; these infants are at the greatest risk for significant kidney disease, which may require surgical correction. SFU grades 3 and 4. 
  • Persistent moderate to severe hydronephrosis requires VCUG to rule out VUR (9%). If the VCUG does not show VUR, further evaluation is dependent on the degree of hydronephrosis (same like bilateral hydronephrosis). 
  • Persistent Severe hydronephrosis requires referral for VCUG and MAG3

DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • The patient afebrile for at least 24 hours
  • Can tolerate oral intake and oral medications
  • Appropriate oral antibiotics are available

DISCHARGE PLAN/INSTRUCTIONS

  • Radiology investigations arranged
  • Follow-up appointment arranged
  • Patient/Family education performed
  • Discharge summary completed

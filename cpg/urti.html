Upper respiratory tract infection

DEFINITIONS

Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI): is a general name for many diseases
The aim in this protocol to discuss:

  1. Common Cold
  2. Bacterial tonsillitis
  3. Otitis Media
  4. Acute Sinusitis
  5. Croup

Common Cold

DEFINITION: Viral infection of nasal and pharyngeal mucosa

ETIOLOGY

  • Mainly Rhinoviruses (200 types, 50 % of common cold) but it can be due other viruses like
    • Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)
    • Influenza viruses
    • Parainfluenza viruses
    • Adenoviruses
    • others

CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

  • Onset of symptoms occurs 1-3 days after viral infection.
  • First symptom is sore or scratchy throat then nasal obstruction and rhinorrhea.
  • Cough (âˆ¼30% of colds)
  • Fever (less in Rhinoviruses)
  • Anterior cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Conjunctival injection
  • Headache and myalgia are absent or mild.
  • Duration: 1 week (10% last for 2 weeks)

PHYSICAL FINDINGS

  • Nasal secretions
  • Erythematous nasal turbinates and pharynx
  • Tonsillar follicles mainly indicate bacterial infection
  • Exclude otitis media during the physical examination

PERSISTENT COMMON COLD SYMPTOMS

  • Allergic rhinitis: Prominent itching and sneezing
  • Foreign body: Unilateral, foul-smelling secretions
  • Sinusitis: Presence of fever, headache or facial pain, or periorbital edema
  • Bacterial Rhinitis: Mucopurulent nasal discharge that excoriates the nares
  • Pertussis: persistent or severe cough

LABORATORY FINDINGS

  • Routine laboratory studies are not helpful for the diagnosis and management of the common cold.
  • Influenza screening can be used if there is a plan to treat influenza with Oseltamivir

TREATMENT

  • Symptomatic treatment is the main stay of management
    • Fever management
    • Nasal care (nasal spray) can improve nasal symptoms
    • Oral hydration
  • No role nebulized mist (WHO)
  • No need for Antiviral treatment
    • The only exception is oseltamivir for influenza
    • More effective is started within the first 48 hours
  • No benefit of antibacterial therapy
  • Cough and cold products (Antitussives and decongestants)
    • Not be used for infants and children younger than 6 years of age
    • 6 to 12 years better not to be used due to lack of effectiveness
    • Adolescents ≥12 years: decongestants may provide symptomatic relief of nasal symptoms
  • Codeine or dextromethorphan hydrobromide has no effect on cough from colds (Controlled medications by MOH)
  • First-generation antihistamines reduce rhinorrhea by 25-30%, (due to their anticholinergic rather than the antihistaminic properties)
  • Second generation antihistamines have no effect on common cold symptoms.
  • Vitamin C, guaifenesin, are not effective
  • Honey can be used for treating cough in children ≥1 year with the common cold
  • Bronchodilators (Salbutamol) are not effective and cause side effects
  • Nebulized steroids (Budesonide) are not effective and harmful

FAMILY EDUCATION

  • Cough clears secretions from the respiratory tract and suppression of cough may result in retention of secretions and potentially harmful airway obstruction
  • Cough and cold products are not safe medication and mortality and morbidity cases reported from their side effects

COMPLICATIONS

  • Otitis media
  • Sinusitis (mostly self-limited, but 5-13% in children are complicated by acute bacterial sinusitis).
  • Exacerbation of asthma

Pharyngotonsillitis

DEFINITION: Acute inflammation of the pharynx including erythema, edema, exudates or enanthem (ulcers and vesicles)

ETIOLOGY

  • Acute Infection
    • Mostly viral
    • Adenovirus usually is seen in summer
    • EBV cause lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, and lymphocytosis
  • Group A β-hemolytic streptococcus (GABHS) is the most common bacteria
    • Rare before 2-3 years of age
    • Peak incidence in school-age children 5-11 years of age
    • Mainly winter and early spring
  • Chronic Infection
    • β-lactamaseproducing organisms (streptococci, Haemophilus influenzae, Peptostreptococcus, Prevotella, and Fusobacterium)
  • Pharyngitis can be due to
    • Fungal infection
    • Noninfectious causes
    • Irritants, smoking, etc.
    • Gastroesophageal reflux
    • Postnasal drip

SYMPTOMS & SIGNS OF ACUTE INFECTION (GABHS)

  • Odynophagia, dry throat, malaise, fever and chills, dysphagia, referred otalgia, headache, muscular aches, and enlarged cervical nodes.
  • Dry tongue, erythematous enlarged tonsils, tonsillar or pharyngeal exudate, palatine petechiae, and enlargement and tenderness of the jugulodigastric lymph nodes

SYMPTOMS & SIGNS OF CHRONIC INFECTION (NOT GABHS)

  • Halitosis, chronic sore throats, foreign body sensation
  • Expelling foul-tasting and foul-smelling cheesy lumps
  • Streptococcal culture is usually negative.

DIAGNOSIS

  • Swab culture (gold standard) [>10 colonies of GABHS]
    • Sensitivity of 90%-95%
    • false negative 5%-10% even without antibiotics
    • Some people are carriers
  • Rapid antigen detection testing (RADT)
    • Sensitivity (70%) specificity (98%)
  • Physicians relying solely on clinical judgment often overestimate the likelihood of a streptococcal etiology
  • EBV infectious mononucleosis
    • CBC with atypical lymphocytes and a positive spot test help to confirm a clinical diagnosis

MANAGEMENT

  • Untreated streptococcal pharyngitis resolves in few days
  • Antibiotic hastens recovery by 12-24 hours
  • Antibiotics prevent rheumatic fever
    • Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/day, (maximum 1 g) given orally for 10 days.
    • Azithromycin: 12 mg/kg day one and 6 mg/kg days 2-5, maximum daily dose 500 mg
    • Clarithromycin: 15 mg/kg/day divided bid for 10 days, maximum dose 250 mg bid
  • Carrier eradication management
    • Cephalosporins (first or second generation) for 10 days.

TONSILLECTOMY

  • For children with recurrent, culture-positive GABHS pharyngitis that has been severe and frequent (>7 episodes in the previous year, or >5 in each of the preceding 2 yrs.).

ADENOIDECTOMY

ADENOIDECTOMY

  • Chronic adenoiditis
  • Chronic sinus infections that have failed medical management
  • Recurrent bouts of acute otitis media

COMPLICATIONS

  • post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
  • acute rheumatic fever
  • Peritonsillar Infection and abscess
  • Parapharyngeal Space Infection
  • Septic thrombophlebitis of the jugular vein

Otitis Media

DEFINITION

  • Acute otitis media (AOM)
  • Otitis media with effusion (OME).

MIDDLE-EAR EFFUSION (MEE)

  • It is a feature both of AOM and of OME
  • The presence of MEE, indicated by any of the following:
    • Bulging of the TM
    • Limited or absent mobility of the TM
    • Air-fluid level behind the TM
    • Otorrhea

DISTINGUISHING BETWEEN AOM AND OME

  • Purulent otorrhea, bulging tympanic membrane and Ear pain indicate AOM
  • air-fluid levels or air bubbles indicate OME
  • OME in the absence of acute infection does not require antimicrobial therapy.

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

  • Irritability
  • Change in sleeping
  • Holding or tugging at the ear (Pulling at the ear has a low sensitivity and specificity)
  • Fever
  • Rupture of the tympanic membrane with purulent otorrhea is uncommon
  • Systemic symptoms associated with upper respiratory tract infections
  • Balance difficulties
  • Mild discomfort or a sense of fullness in the ear (older children)

TYMPANIC MEMBRANE FINDINGS

  • Contour: (normally slightly concave) fullness, bulging, or conversely extreme retraction
  • Color: (normally pearly gray)
    • Erythema (intense)
    • Erythema alone may result from crying or vascular flushing
    • Whitening may result from either scarring or the presence of liquid in the middle-ear cavity
  • Translucency (Translucent): opacification denotes scarring or effusion
  • Structural changes: scars, perforations, retraction pockets,
  • Mobility: (mobile) most sensitive and specific in determining the presence or absence of MEE

DIAGNOSIS

  • A certain diagnosis of OM should contain all of the following elements:
    • Recent and usually acute onset of illness
    • Presence of MEE
    • Signs and symptoms of middle-ear inflammation including erythema of the tympanic membrane or otalgia.
  • Tympanometry offers objective evidence of the presence or absence of MEE.
  • Tympanogram provides information about tympanic membrane (TM) compliance

TREATMENT

  • Patients <6 months: treat all presumed cases
  • Patients <2 yr. of age: treat all confirmed diagnoses cases
  • Patients > 2 years old: observation may be considered (in all episodes of non-severe OM) for 2-3 days with close follow-up, if lack of spontaneous resolution or worsening of symptoms to start treatment
  • Recommended antibacterial agents
    • First line Amoxicillin 80-90 mg/kg/day for 10 days
    • Second-Line amoxicillin-clavulanate, cefdinir, cefuroxime axetil, and intramuscular ceftriaxone (Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg/day for 3 days)
  • Penicillin Allergy
    • if non-type 1 allergy: cefdinir, cefuroxime
    • if type 1 allergy: azithromycin or clarithromycin
  • Indications of Myringotomy and Tympanocentesis in AOM
    • Severe refractory pain
    • Hyperpyrexia
    • Mastoiditis
    • Labyrinthitis,
    • Central nervous system infection
    • Immunologic compromise
    • Failed 2 courses of antibiotics
    • Neonates < 2 weeks
  • Changing Antibiotics
    • Persistent or recurrent ear pain or fever or both after 2-3 days of therapy
    • Development of a suppurative complication
    • When a patient fails to respond to amoxicillin, neither trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole nor erythromycin sulfisoxazole is optimal
    • A three-day course of parenteral ceftriaxone because of its superior efficacy against S. pneumoniae
    • Tympanocentesis should be performed to make a bacteriologic diagnosis
    • If tympanocentesis cannot be performed, a course of clindamycin may be considered for the rare case of penicillin-resistant pneumococcal infection
    • If the patient fails to respond to clindamycin, tympanocentesis is necessary

COMPLICATIONS OF ACUTE OTITIS MEDIA

  • Intratemporal complication
  • Infectious dermatitis
  • Tympanic membrane perforation
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM)
  • Acute mastoiditis
  • Facial paralysis
  • Acquired cholesteatoma
  • Labyrinthitis

REFERRAL TO ENT

  • Persistent ear discharge
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media
  • Effusion for more than three months
  • Suspicion of hearing loss
  • Balance abnormalities
  • Persistent tinnitus

Sinusitis

DEFINITION: Sinusitis is inflammation of the lining of the paranasal sinuses with significant acute and chronic morbidity as well as the potential for serious complications.

ETIOLOGY

  • Viral (self-limited rhino sinusitis)
  • Bacterial caused by
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae (∼30%)
    • nontypable Haemophilus influenzae (∼20%)
    • Moraxella catarrhalis (∼20%).

CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

  • Nonspecific complaints
  • Nasal congestion
  • Purulent nasal discharge (unilateral or bilateral)
  • Fever and cough.
  • Halitosis
  • Hyposmia
  • Periorbital edema.
  • Headache and facial pain are rare in children.
  • Children with chronic sinusitis have a history of persistent respiratory symptoms, including cough, nasal discharge, or nasal congestion, lasting >90 days.

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION

  • Erythema and swelling of the nasal mucosa
  • Purulent nasal discharge.
  • Sinus tenderness
  • Transillumination reveals an opaque sinus that transmits light poorly.

DIAGNOSIS

  • History.
  • Nasal discharge and cough, for >10-14 days without improvement,
  • Fever of at least (39C) and purulent nasal discharge for 3-4 consecutive days, suggest a complicating acute bacterial sinusitis.
  • Sinus aspirate culture is the only accurate method of diagnosis
  • Radiographic studies (sinus plain films, CT scans) including opacification, mucosal thickening, or presence of an air-fluid level are not totally diagnostic

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

  • Viral URTI
  • Allergic rhinitis,
  • Nonallergic rhinitis,
  • Nasal foreign body

TREATMENT

  • Amoxicillin: (45 mg/kg/day) is adequate for the majority of children with uncomplicated acute bacterial sinusitis.
  • Alternatives
    • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole,
    • Cefuroxime axetil,
    • Clarithromycin or azithromycin.

THE USE OF DECONGESTANTS

  • Antihistamines, mucolytic, and intranasal corticosteroids not recommended for the treatment of acute uncomplicated bacterial sinusitis.
  • Saline nasal washes or nasal sprays can help to liquefy secretions and act as a mild vasoconstrictor, but the effects have not been systematically evaluated in children

COMPLICATIONS

  • Periorbital cellulitis
  • Orbital cellulitis
  • Ethmoiditis.
  • Epidural abscess
  • Meningitis,
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis,
  • Subdural empyema,
  • Brain abscess
  • Osteomyelitis of the frontal bone

Croup

DEFINITION: viral infection of the glottic and subglottic regions

ETIOLOGY

  • Croup is usually caused by viruses (65% Parainfluenza virus)
    • Parainfluenza virus type 1
      • the most common cause
      • Cause fall and winter epidemics
    • Parainfluenza type 2 causes croup outbreaks, but milder disease
    • Parainfluenza type 3 causes sporadic cases, but more severe
  • Bacterial infection may occur secondarily.

EPIDEMIOLOGY

  • most commonly occurs in children 6-36 months of age
  • uncommon in children >6 years old
  • Family history of croup is a risk factor for croup and recurrent croup.
  • Most cases of croup occur in the fall or early winter
  • ED visits for croup are most frequent between 10:00 PM-4:00 AM
  • Children seen for croup between noon and 6:00 PM are more likely to be admitted to the hospital
  • Another peak visit occurs in the morning 7:00 AM-11:00 AM
  • 5% of croup patients required hospital admission
  • 4% among those discharged home, had a repeat ED visit within 48 hours

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

  • Barking cough (the diagnostic symptom)
  • Hoarseness of voice
  • Inspiratory stridor
  • Fever from afebrile to low-grade to 39-40 â—¦C
  • Can be preceded by Rhinorrhea and Pharyngitis
  • Symptoms worsen at night
  • Agitation and crying greatly aggravate the symptoms and signs

EXAMINATION

  • Respiratory distress Mild to Severe proportional to airway narrowing
  • Stridor can decrease in severe narrowing
  • Low sPO2 in severe narrowing

INVESTIGATIONS

  • Mostly not needed since croup can be diagnosed clinically
  • Neck X-ray: may be normal in patients with croup, narrowing in X-ray is not correlated with the clinical condition

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

  • Bacterial tracheitis
  • Foreign body
  • Retropharyngeal or peritonsillar abscess
  • Angioedema

TREATMENT

  • Humidified oxygen to keep saturation around 95%
  • Nebulized L-Epinephrine
    • 1 mg/mL (1:1000)
    • Dose: 0.5 mL/kg per dose (max. doses: ≤4 yr: 2.5 mL/dose; >4 yr: 5 mL/dose)
    • nebulizer over 15 minutes
  • Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone)
    • Single dose
    • 0.15-0.6 mg (maximum 10 mg)
    • Better to be oral or IV (not IM)
    • Effect lasts 2-3 days
    • Alternatively, one dose of nebulized budesonide (2 mg) can be used
  • Cool mist (No evidence)
  • Antibiotics (if bacterial infection suspected)

RECURRENT CROUP

  1. Viral
  2. Subglottic stenosis (prior intubation, congenital subglottic stenosis, previous airway procedure)
  3. Reflux esophagitis
  4. Eosinophilic esophagitis
  5. candidal esophagitis
  6. Asthma
  7. Seasonal allergies
  8. Subglottic cyst, and hemangioma
